# The 15 Principles

12 psychological + 3 technical. Every high-converting quiz respects these.

## Psychological

### 1. Micro-commitments (the commitment cascade)

Each question answered is a small "yes" that makes the next one easier. Every step forward increases sunk cost and makes abandonment psychologically painful. This is why Q1 must be trivially easy — the first click is the most valuable one.

### 2. Problem reframing (blame shift)

Shift responsibility from willpower to biology/chemistry/mechanism. **"It's not your fault"** removes shame and makes the solution feel necessary, not optional.

Example: "Your weight isn't about willpower — it's a damaged metabolism from years of restrictive dieting."

### 3. Self-diagnosis (internal conviction)

Users convince **themselves** they have a problem by checking symptom boxes. The more boxes checked, the deeper their belief in needing help. Multi-select questions weaponize this — every checkmark is a micro-confession.

### 4. Progressive problem amplification

Start with presence → move to severity → end with life impact. Each layer makes the problem feel bigger and more urgent. A vague "I have fungus" becomes "I have moderate severity on 3-5 nails with discoloration, flaking, and odor." **Quantified problems feel urgent.**

### 5. Personalization theatre

Loading screens and dynamic content create the illusion of bespoke analysis. Even template-based results feel custom when they reference user data. **Less is more** — a few specific callbacks (age, chosen goal) beat heavy-handed personalization.

### 6. Educational authority

Info slides between questions teach the mechanism while building credibility. Science + citations = trust. Trust = willingness to buy. These slides destroy competing solutions without naming a single competitor.

### 7. Social proof integration

Testimonials and "X people joined" counters normalize the problem and solution. If 400,000 people did it, it must be safe and effective. **Place social proof BEFORE deep questions** — trust enables vulnerability.

### 8. The progress effect

Progress bars create momentum and make abandonment feel like wasted effort. "You're 80% done!" leverages sunk cost to push completion. Always show progress after a few questions.

### 9. Identity priming

Questions about goals and motivations make users visualize their future self. "How motivated are you?" forces them to declare high motivation, which then primes consistent action. Identity-level answers ("I am someone who...") are far stickier than behavioral answers.

### 10. Urgency without fake scarcity

Biological deadlines ("nerve repair window closing") feel more real than stock limits. Real events ("Do you have an important event coming up?" — wedding, vacation, reunion) create **personal** time pressure. Time pressure that comes from THEIR situation beats fake countdown timers every time.

### 11. The curiosity gap

Gating results behind email creates irresistible need to know. After investing 5 minutes, users MUST see "their personalized plan." The gap — not the benefit — is what drives the email hand-over.

### 12. Symptom clustering (multi-select strategy)

Multi-select lets users acknowledge multiple problems at once. More symptoms selected = bigger problem = more desperate for solution. Use multi-select for symptom and pain-point questions. Single-select for segmentation (age, gender, goal) and identity.

## Technical

### 13. One question per screen

No scrolling. No distractions. No "submit" button for multi-step forms. Each question is its own commitment moment. Mobile-first by default — the vast majority of quiz traffic is phone.

### 14. Question-type variety (fatigue prevention)

Never use the same question type more than twice in a row. Rotate:

- Yes/No
- Multi-select
- Single-select
- Agree-scale (statements to confirm)
- Slider/rating (1-5)
- Emoji tap
- Text input (use sparingly, forces cognitive load)

Long quizzes (30+ questions) live or die on this rotation.

### 15. Strategic info-slide placement

Info slides (the "breather" / "insight panel") belong **BETWEEN question clusters**, not before or after the quiz. Use them to:

- Release pressure after a heavy admission
- Reframe competing solutions
- Introduce a new mechanism
- Normalize the user's experience

Rule of thumb: one insight panel for every 4-5 questions.

### 16. Drop-off Diagnosis (per-question CVR tracking)

You cannot fix what you do not measure. Every question in your quiz has its own drop-off rate. The key insight: **find the ONE question killing your funnel and split-test until it normalizes**.

Real example (The Funnel Professor): a quiz funnel went from 19% completion to 25% by fixing one question. Original Q4: "What solutions have you tried before?" with too many options = 22% drop-off. Simplified to 4 options + "None of the above" = 9% drop-off. 6% more completions = thousands more buyers.

**Benchmarks to track per question:**

| Question | Normal drop-off |
|---|---|
| Q1 (entry) | 30-40% (this is the biggest filter, expected) |
| Q2-Qn | Under 15% each |
| Loading screens | Should be near 0% (they can't click away) |
| Email gate | 20-30% is normal |
| Overall completion | Aim for 25%+ on cold traffic |

**Fix strategy:**

If any single non-entry question has 10%+ drop-off, that's your priority A/B test. Common fixes:

- Too many options → simplify to 4 max + "None of the above"
- Question feels too personal too early → move it later in the arc
- Confusing wording → rewrite for 5th-grade reading level
- Long input field (text/date) → replace with multi-choice if possible

**Don't optimize for 100% completion.** High-converting quiz funnels deliberately filter. 25% completion at 5% offer-page CVR beats 80% completion at 0.5% offer-page CVR. Depth beats volume.

### 17. Low-Friction Entry Rule

The single most counter-intuitive principle in quiz funnels:

> Don't try to hook them with your first question. Get them clicking first. Hook them once they're in.

Most marketers think Q1 needs to be powerful, aspirational, emotionally charged. "What do you want to achieve?" sounds like a great opener.

**Wrong.** Look at every high-converting quiz funnel - Bioma, Nooro, Lunavia, SkinnyFit, Spartan, Raising.dog, EveryDoggy, Woofz, PawChamp - they ALL start the same way:

> Question 1: Age or gender.

That's it. The most boring question imaginable. **Why this works:**

- **Zero cognitive load** - "How old are you?" requires no thinking. They just click.
- **Starts the yes-chain** - one click in, they're committed. Quitting now feels like wasted effort.
- **Sets up personalization** - implied promise: "we need this to tailor your results." Now every question feels customized.

The aspirational stuff - "What do you want to achieve?" - belongs at Q3 or Q4. By then they're already invested. The emotional questions hit harder because they've committed to the process.

**The proven entry sequence:**

| # | Question | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | Age range | Single-select, 4-5 buckets |
| Q2 | Gender (or second demographic) | Single-select |
| Q3 | Goals/desires (NOW you go aspirational) | Multi-select |
| Q4 | Pain points/struggles | Multi-select |
| Q5+ | Deeper diagnosis, life events, behavior | Mixed formats |

Reverse the order at your peril. Front-loading aspirational questions kills Q1 completion.

## The Meta-Rule

These 15 principles exist in tension with each other. You can't just add every technique and hope for the best. A good quiz funnel picks its spots:

- **Cold + problem-aware audience** → lean on principles 1, 3, 4, 6 (commitment, diagnosis, amplification, authority). Longer quiz (10-30 questions).
- **Warm + solution-aware audience** → lean on principles 9, 11, 12 (identity, curiosity, clustering). Shorter quiz (7-12 questions).
- **Identity-based products** (ADHD, beauty, lifestyle) → heavy on 2 and 9 (reframing, identity priming).
- **Physical-condition products** (collagen, joint pain, fungus) → heavy on 4 and 10 (amplification, biological urgency).
