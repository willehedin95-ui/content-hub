# The Villain Framework

How to find your enemy, side with your market, and build unshakeable trust.

Source: The Funnel Professor (@DTC_Quizbuilder), Jan 21 2026.

---

## The core thesis

Every great brand has an enemy. Apple had IBM. Nike had laziness. Dave Ramsey has debt.

But here's what most marketers miss: **the villain isn't a competitor. It's not a person you can name. It's a system. An industry. A lie your customer has been told their whole life.**

When you name that villain, something powerful happens. You stop being a salesperson. You become an ally. You're not pitching - you're fighting alongside them.

That's the difference between a customer and a fan. Between a transaction and a tribe.

## Why villains work (Donald Miller's StoryBrand)

Every story has 4 characters:

- **The Hero** - your customer. They want something.
- **The Guide** - you. You help them get it.
- **The Victim** - who they're afraid of becoming.
- **The Villain** - what's holding them back.

Most brands position themselves as the hero ("we're the best"). Nobody cares. Your customer is the hero of their own story.

When you attack a villain, you prove you understand. You're saying: "I see the same enemy you see. I've been fighting it too. Let me show you how to win."

That's trust you can't buy with ad spend.

## The 7 villain archetypes

### Archetype 1: The Greedy Industry

The entire industry profits from keeping customers stuck.

**How it sounds:**
- "Big Pharma doesn't want you healthy."
- "The diet industry profits from your yo-yo cycle."
- "Dentists make money from repeat visits, not cures."

**Why it works:** Customers already feel this frustration. You're giving voice to what they already believe. You're not creating distrust - you're validating it.

**Best for:** Health, wellness, supplements, financial services, insurance.

### Archetype 2: The Gatekeepers

Experts, professionals, or institutions that hoard knowledge to maintain power.

**How it sounds:**
- "Wall Street doesn't want you to know this."
- "Doctors are trained to prescribe, not prevent."
- "Marketing agencies keep you dependent on purpose."

**Why it works:** Positions your product as insider access. The customer gets to bypass the gatekeepers and go direct.

**Best for:** Finance, investing, marketing education, DIY solutions.

### Archetype 3: The Old Way

Outdated methods, conventional wisdom, or "how it's always been done."

**How it sounds:**
- "Calorie counting doesn't work anymore."
- "Cold calling is dead."
- "Traditional advertising is a waste of money."

**Why it works:** Customers have tried the old way and failed. Blaming the method (not them) removes shame and opens them to a new approach.

**Best for:** Any market with established competitors or legacy solutions.

### Archetype 4: The Misleading Guru

Fake experts who sold them bad advice.

**How it sounds:**
- "Forget everything the fitness influencers told you."
- "Most business coaches have never built a business."
- "The gurus selling courses can't do what they teach."

**Why it works:** Positions you as the real deal. The authentic practitioner vs the polished pretender.

**Best for:** Coaching, courses, info products, B2B services.

### Archetype 5: The Rigged System

A larger system designed to keep them down.

**How it sounds:**
- "The algorithm is burying your content."
- "The tax code is designed for the wealthy."
- "The hiring system filters out the best candidates."

**Why it works:** Externalizes failure. It's not their fault - the game is rigged. Your product is the workaround.

**Best for:** Social media, career, finance, small business.

### Archetype 6: The Hidden Cause

The real problem no one talks about.

**How it sounds:**
- "It's not your metabolism. It's your gut bacteria."
- "It's not your ads. It's your landing page."
- "It's not your willpower. It's your hormones."

**Why it works:** Reframes the problem entirely. Creates an "aha" moment. If the cause is different, the solution must be different too.

**Best for:** Health, supplements, marketing, productivity, training/behavior.

### Archetype 7: The Complicated Solution

The current solutions are too complex, expensive, or time-consuming.

**How it sounds:**
- "You don't need a 47-step funnel."
- "You don't need to spend 2 hours in the gym."
- "You don't need a $10,000 agency retainer."

**Why it works:** Positions your solution as the simple alternative. The shortcut. The hack.

**Best for:** SaaS, services, courses, any market with complex competitors.

## Real examples from 8-figure funnels

### Steel Bite Pro - Villain: Big Dental ($100M+)

> "Dentists don't want to cure your tooth problems. They want you coming back every six months. They treat symptoms because healthy patients don't pay bills."

The product? A supplement that "addresses the root cause" of dental issues. Without Big Dental as the enemy it's just another supplement. With the villain it's a rebellion.

### Dan Kennedy - Villain: Madison Avenue ($100M+)

> "Madison Avenue agencies win awards. They don't win customers. They create clever ads that entertain. I create ugly ads that sell."

His audience: small business owners burned by agencies. Kennedy didn't compete with agencies - he declared war on them. His customers became soldiers in that war.

### Dave Ramsey - Villain: Debt Culture

> "Banks want you in debt. Credit card companies profit from your minimum payments. Society tells you debt is normal. It's not. It's slavery."

The villain creates the tribe.

### Bioma - Villain: Failed Diets

The Bioma quiz funnel asks about every diet you've tried (Keto, Paleo, Fasting, Calorie counting). Then delivers the verdict:

> "No wonder nothing worked. These diets ignore the real problem - your gut bacteria. You were never broken. Your microbiome was."

The villain is every diet that made you feel like a failure.

### Alex Hormozi - Villain: Overcomplication

> "You don't need a complicated funnel. You need an offer so good people feel stupid saying no."
> "Stop adding tactics. Start removing friction."

The villain is the guru-industrial complex selling 47-step systems.

## How to find your villain (5-step process)

### Step 1: List what they've tried

Write down every solution your customer has attempted before finding you.
- What diets have they tried?
- What software have they used?
- What advice have they followed?
- What professionals have they hired?

These failed attempts are where your villain lives.

### Step 2: Identify who they blame

When your customers complain, who or what do they blame?
- "My metabolism is broken."
- "The algorithm hates me."
- "Agencies just take your money."
- "Doctors don't listen."

Listen to their language. The villain is already in their vocabulary.

### Step 3: Find the system that profits

Ask: Who benefits from my customer staying stuck?
- Diet industry profits from repeat customers.
- Pharma profits from ongoing prescriptions.
- Agencies profit from client dependency.
- Banks profit from debt.

Follow the money. The villain is whoever profits from the problem continuing.

### Step 4: Name the lie

What has your customer been told that isn't true?
- "Eat less, move more."
- "You need money to make money."
- "Content is king."
- "It takes 10 years to build a business."

The lie is often conventional wisdom. Calling it out positions you as the truth-teller.

### Step 5: Craft your villain statement

Use this formula:

> "[Industry/System/Method] wants you to believe [lie] because they profit from [your pain]. The truth is [your mechanism]. That's why [your product] works when nothing else has."

Example: "The diet industry wants you to believe it's about calories because they profit from your yo-yo cycle. The truth is it's about gut bacteria. That's why Bioma works when diets fail."

## The 5 rules of villain marketing

### Rule 1: Never attack a person by name

Attack systems, not individuals. "Big Pharma" not "Dr. Smith." "Madison Avenue" not "Agency X."

Named attacks look petty. Systemic attacks look principled.

### Rule 2: Your customer must already feel this

You're not creating new enemies. You're naming existing frustrations.

If your villain doesn't resonate immediately, it's the wrong villain.

### Rule 3: Make the villain faceless but specific

"The system" is too vague. "The dental industry that profits from repeat visits" is specific.

Specificity creates believability.

### Rule 4: You're the guide, not the hero

You're not defeating the villain for them. You're giving them the weapon to defeat it themselves.

Your product is Excalibur. They're the one who wields it.

### Rule 5: The villain explains why everything else failed

The villain answers the question: "Why hasn't anything worked before?"

Not because you're broken. Because the system was designed to fail you.

That reframe is everything.

## Implementation in quiz funnels

The villain framework slots directly into the quiz arc:

**Phase 1 questions** establish the user's failed attempts ("What have you tried?")

**Pre-frame interstitial** introduces the villain ("Most [solutions] fail because of [villain mechanism]")

**Diagnosis** reframes their failure as the system's fault, not theirs ("It's not you - here's the hidden cause")

**Offer page** positions your product as the weapon to defeat the villain

The strongest villain pattern in quiz funnels is **The Hidden Cause** + **The Old Way** combined - "What you've been told is wrong, here's the real cause, here's the new mechanism."

## The bottom line

Your customer is already angry. They've tried things that didn't work. They've been sold promises that weren't kept. They've blamed themselves when the system was the problem.

Your job isn't to create that anger. Your job is to name it. Validate it. Channel it.

When you do, you stop being a vendor. You become an ally.

And allies get trust that competitors can't buy.
