# Teardown Lessons

Compressed tactical lessons from live high-converting funnels. Each entry: who, what, stealable pattern.

## Spoiled Child (Kilo Health)

Multi 8-9 figure collagen/personalized supplement brand sending 80%+ of traffic to a quiz funnel.

**Stealable:**

- **Open with "Do any of these apply to you?"** symptom multi-select. Every checkbox is a micro-confession that deepens commitment.
- **Protein intake / fitness lifestyle questions** act as status priming — people feel seen and are more willing to follow customized recommendations.
- **Flavor preference question** (mango vs pineapple) — playful, personalizes the tasting experience, makes the user imagine consuming the product.
- **Social proof interlude** mid-quiz: "5 of 6 users saw [outcome]." Progress bar continues so momentum isn't lost.
- **Attribution questions** ("Where did you hear about us?") placed LATE after emotional questions — they don't distract from the journey.
- **$0 now, $55 if kept** offer with 30-day billing. The upsell is a "best results" version for $0 now, $110 if kept.

## Lunavia (toenail fungus)

28-slide quiz with explicit 5-phase structure. Selling a single product, one solution - quiz exists to make the user NEED it before seeing it. The Funnel Professor's canonical case study for **5-phase quiz architecture** (also documented as Framework 3 in 02-arc-and-phases.md).

**Stealable:**

- **Progressive micro-commitment**: easy → emotional. Never jump to emotional before commitment is built. Phase 1 (slides 1-3) opens with gender → age → goal. Zero vulnerability. Just clicks. The completion instinct kicks in.
- **Phase 2 - Problem Diagnosis** (slides 5-13): force the user to confront their condition. Where? How many? How severe? How long? What symptoms? Every question makes it MORE REAL. A vague "I have fungus" becomes "I have moderate severity on 3-5 nails with discoloration, flaking, and odor." Quantified problems feel urgent.
- **The symptom multi-select** (Q9): "Do you experience any of the following?" - 4-6 specific symptoms. Every box checked = a micro-confession. Check 3 of 4 and brain goes "damn, this is worse than I thought." That's the point.
- **Belief-seeding lifestyle questions**: "Closed shoes all day?" → maybe your footwear caused this. "Communal showers often?" → you're still exposed to reinfection. "Family member has it?" → you might be spreading it. Each answer builds the case for treatment NOW.
- **Phase 3 - Behavioral Assessment**: ask about routine frequency. Then IMMEDIATELY drop info slide "It Takes Less Than 60 Seconds a Day." Objection destroyed before it formed.
- **Competitive destruction via new mechanism**: one slide mid-quiz "Why Most Treatments Fail. Most creams stay on the surface, never reaching the infection. Lunavia uses penetrating oils that soften the nail so antifungal agents reach the ROOT." One slide. Every competitor destroyed.
- **Phase 4 - Emotional Escalation** (slides 19-26): the funnel gets aggressive.
  - Q19 shame: "Have you felt embarrassed about showing your nails in public?"
  - Q20 protective instinct: "Do you worry about spreading it to family members?"
  - Protective instinct (kids) is STRONGER than personal shame ("I can live with ugly nails" is easy to say. "I might be giving this to my kids" is not).
- **Motivation anchoring with word choice**: "How motivated are you to **INVEST** time and resources into finally getting rid of this?" INVEST, not SPEND. People who say they're motivated feel compelled to ACT motivated.
- **Time-bound urgency via real events**: "Do you have an important event coming up?" Wedding, vacation, reunion. Real deadlines beat fake countdown timers. Someone with a wedding in 8 weeks doesn't think "maybe someday" - they think "I need to start TODAY."
- **Personalized timeline** on results: "First visible improvements: 5-7 days. Full elimination: estimated [specific date]." Specific dates > vague promises always.
- **Phase 5 - Double Commitment Gate**:
  - Q1: "Are you ready to finally eliminate this for good?"
  - Q2: "Can you commit to applying daily?"
  - Two YES clicks. Saying "no" to the offer page would now be psychologically INCONSISTENT with the commitments. 20-30% CVR boost alone.
- **The 8-technique stack across 28 slides**: progressive micro-commitment / self-diagnosis through quantification / competitive destruction via new mechanism / emotional escalation (shame → fear → protective instinct) / time-bound urgency / strategic social proof timing / diagnostic authority / double commitment gate.

## HikeFootwear (barefoot shoes for 40+ with foot pain)

7 questions + 1 education slide. Problem-aware audience.

**Stealable:**

- **More aware = shorter quiz**. Less aware = longer. HikeFootwear's prospect already knows their feet hurt, so 7 is enough.
- **The three jobs a problem-aware quiz has to do**: validate the problem, disqualify previous solutions, introduce a new mechanism.
- **Confession-then-reframe sequence**: Q4 asks "What have you tried?" → they admit failure. THEN the interstitial drops: "Most 'comfortable' shoes are weakening your feet."
- **Single interstitial reframe can do the work of a 2000-word VSL** because the quiz questions before it pre-loaded the emotional context.
- **Competitive destruction without naming competitors** — the reader makes the connection themselves.
- **Review-mined copy**: testimonials use language like "these **actually** worked" — that's the language of someone burned before.
- **Match testimonials to the audience**: Margaret talks walks with grandkids, Sarah is a nurse, Robert mentions gardening. Not influencers. Everyday mobility stories.

## Javvy (protein coffee - solution-aware audience)

9 steps. Short quiz for warm traffic. The Funnel Professor's canonical example of **how to write a quiz for a solution-aware audience**. About 1/4 of all traffic going to it.

**Stealable:**

- **Solution-aware = shorter quiz**: 9 steps total. Customers know what they're in for, no 30-question warmup needed. The quiz is heavier on close than diagnosis.
- **Steps 1-5 are the foreplay**: "What protein do you use?" / "What are your goals?" - easy, low-threat, zero cognitive load. Not segmenting - building micro-commitments. Every click is a tiny "yes" that makes the next click easier.
- **AOV anchoring via self-prescription** (Q4-5): "How many coffees per day?" and "How much protein per serving?" look like personalization but are actually bundle-size anchors. User just told the quiz they drink 3 coffees and need 2 scoops. Now the bundle price on the results page feels justified - because they prescribed it to themselves.
- **Step 6 is the pivot - full-screen agitation interstitial**: No question. "What's REALLY in your coffee and protein shakes? Sugar. Fake flavors. Junk. Cheap incomplete proteins." Five clicks deep and NOW the reader is told their current routine is broken. Classic problem agitation that hits harder because they just spent 2 minutes describing that routine in detail.
- **"Shameful No" closing sequence (steps 7-9)**: three rapid yes/no questions where the NO options are deliberately embarrassing:
  - "No, I love my unhealthy habits"
  - "No, I love overpaying for coffee"
  - "No, I love wasting time in lines"
  - Nobody clicks those. They're not real options - they're a forcing function. You click YES because the alternative makes you look like an idiot even to yourself.
- **The shameful no pre-handles objections**: three yeses in a row → taste objection handled, price objection handled, convenience objection handled. Then the offer drops with 58% off, 4 free gifts, today only.
- **Two master techniques to steal**:
  1. **Shameful no** - frame your "no" answer as an identity nobody wants to claim. Fence-sitters convert because inaction feels worse than action.
  2. **Self-prescribed quantity anchoring** - let the quiz do the upselling. When the customer tells YOU how much they need before seeing the offer, bundle pricing feels like confirmation, not a pitch.

## Liven (mental wellness / overthinking)

30-question quiz. Heavy use of agree-scale statements. Identity-based product.

**Stealable:**

- **5-stage psych funnel**: Self-Label → Pain Deepen → Cost Expand → Hope Reframe → Future Cast (pseudo-diagnosis).
- **Agree-scale STATEMENTS, not questions**: "I find myself stuck on the same thoughts" forces a Yes more strongly than "Do you overthink?"
- **Timed hope injection** after pain/cost: "87% of overthinkers are more empathetic and intelligent." Perfectly placed after 18 questions of making you feel broken.
- **Pseudo-diagnosis result visuals**: Score. Type. Color-coded severity. Clinical aesthetic. Not a quiz result — a DIAGNOSIS. Prescription loads next.
- **Format rotation** is critical for 30+ question funnels: yes/no → multi-select → emoji scales → 1-5 ratings → statement agreement. Never same type more than twice in a row.

## Welcome Baby (personalized baby books)

Not a problem/solution product. Emotional/gift category. The Funnel Professor case study for **how to do quiz funnels for products without a huge pain point**.

**Stealable:**

- **First question isn't about the product** - "Has your baby arrived yet, or are you still expecting?" Emotional activation before data collection. You just made a new parent think about their baby. Game over.
- **The quiz is about the customer, not the product.** Each question pulls them deeper into their own story, not into product specs.
- **Self vs gift segmentation Q1** when product is gift-capable. Splits into two tonal funnels with different intent and phrasing - "almost 2 funnels in 1."
- **Future pacing with authority**: question 10 introduces "games designed by professionals" - elevates the product from sentimental keepsake to parenting tool. New value layer unlocked mid-quiz.
- **The quiz is the product, not the sales mechanism**: by Q5, "you're not evaluating a product. You're imagining your family's book." Questions about gender reveals, first haircut, handprints make the user mentally flip through a book that doesn't exist yet.
- **Self-argued purchase**: nobody tells the user "you can track milestones" or "keep memories." The user tells themselves through their answers. "You're arguing with yourself on why you should buy this product."
- **Late-quiz confession deepening**: questions 11-15 go deep on baby's personality, activities, development focus. "This isn't a quiz - it's a confession. The more you share, the more tailored the result feels. The more tailored it feels, the harder it is to leave."

## Happy Mammoth

"Obvious" quiz-first funnel. Ad CTA is "Take Quiz" or "Get Your Diagnosis Free." The Funnel Professor's example of running quiz funnels with EXPLICIT quiz-CTA ads (vs disguising them as normal landing pages).

**Stealable:**

- **Position quiz as free value, not effort**: "Get your diagnosis free" > "Take our quiz." Cheaper clicks in ads because user perceives value, not extraction. "The goal of an ad is to get the click - not sell."
- **The "obvious quiz" ad strategy**: when ad CTA is explicitly "Take Quiz," click-through soars but AOV stays the same because the customer is qualified through the quiz before reaching the offer. Increasing frequency of these ad styles in their library suggests it's working.
- **On-brand landing page** matching the ad's vibe (header + footer clickable on Happy Mammoth though - the Funnel Professor flags this as a potential drop-off issue worth A/B testing).
- **Skip the gradualism rule**: customers know they're entering a quiz. Don't waste time. Dive into frustrating symptoms quickly (still not too invasive).
- **"Doctor's diagnosis" format**: ask a bunch of questions slowly, never prescribe immediately. The patient trusts the prescription because they trust the process. Sit them in the doctor's chair, M+R along the way.
- **Very specific daily-life questions** ("How is your energy at 3pm?") hit harder than generic symptoms. "Finally someone knows what I'm going through" builds trust.
- **Self-admittance is the master lever**: questions like "Do you struggle to sleep?" → user answers Yes. They told you. You don't need to convince them they have a problem. They told you. The most powerful psychological lever behind the quiz.
- **Avatar bucket data internally** for retargeting + email segmentation per primary problem.
- **Where Happy Mammoth could improve** (per Funnel Professor): the diagnosis page doesn't seem to change based on answers. Personalized result pages by primary pain would lift CVR. Also no clear scroll-to-offer signal for users (sub-optimal UX).

## Mars Men

Multi-9-figure men's health (assumed $100M+ annual run rate). 1.5M+ visitors on similar web, most going to a quiz funnel. Quiz framed as "2-minute assessment." The Funnel Professor's canonical example of the **3-card closing system**.

**Stealable:**

- **Frame quiz as assessment giving value**: "Find out what's happening - this 2-minute assessment analyzes your symptoms, lifestyle, and risk factors in order to find a solution." User feels they're getting something (free assessment) before making an ask. Build rapport, feel indebted, all in 2 minutes before any pitch.
- **Multiple ad angles routing to ONE quiz**: TRT / weight loss / will-power / aging / better GLP-1 performance. Each ad has its angle and visual call-out, but all route through the same quiz that takes them from problem-aware to most-aware via psychological progression.
- **The "doctor question"**: hyper-specific symptoms only the avatar would recognize. "Energy levels at 3pm?" If someone has this issue, they think "Finally, someone knows what I'm going through." Builds massive trust.
- **3-card system in results** (the canonical Mars Men close):
  1. **Profile card**: all your answers - shows how cooked you are (urgency + fear)
  2. **Future Pacing card**: where you can be if you make a change today, with graph showing trajectory split (with vs without intervention) + specific date ("By Feb 6")
  3. **Offer card**: based on all your answers, this solution is perfect for you
- **Future pacing with time specificity**: "By Feb 6th" works better than "in 90 days." Specific dates feel like forecasts, vague timelines feel like marketing.
- **Claim-your-offer loading screen** at the transition between results and offer. 3-5 second micro-loader. Anticipation peaks right before the price reveal.
- **The doctor analogy**: "You don't question the doc when he writes your prescription? Same with the quiz. He asked questions, M+R'd along the way, and now you trust the prescription is personalized for your problem."
- **Avatar buckets baked into routing**: each answer combination maps to a different avatar with different buying intent. Internally tracked for retargeting + email segmentation.
- **Future pacing graph essentials**: red warning level showing current trajectory + green growth showing intervention trajectory + specific date marker. Two lines, one chart, complete sales pitch.

## Seranova Beauty (Heyflow 24-screen quiz)

Wrinkles/collagen for older women.

**Stealable:**

- **Face images on age question**: each age bracket shows a model photo. Big CVR lift on beauty/aging products.
- **Science interstitials with charts**: "Your Collagen Levels Drop Every Year" with a graph showing the decline. Plants urgency with biology.

## Nutrops (nootropics for grandmothers)

Narrow demographic targeting. Story-led landing page, not quiz-led.

**Stealable lesson for quiz positioning:**

- **Lead with identity, not product**: "The Grandma Who's Really There" > "Nootropic Mushroom Gummies." User arrives in their emotional world before they see a supplement.
- **Messenger matches the market**: Sherry (70, grandmother, active) — not a model. Trust built through resemblance, not authority.
- **Speak the awareness stage perfectly**: audience isn't searching for "nootropics," they're noticing afternoon brain fog. Speak to noticed symptoms, not clinical ingredients.

## Primal Viking / Hollow Sox (listicle + quiz for new-mechanism products)

New mechanism in a saturated category (reindeer organs vs beef, alpaca fiber vs synthetic).

**Stealable:**

- **Comparison table above the fold**: side-by-side on key attributes. Anchors the page. You don't have to convince them the category works — they already believe it. Convince them they picked the wrong KIND.
- **Listicle format** ("10 Reasons") with competitive destruction per point. Each reason = alternative destroyed.
- **Awareness progression**: problem-aware → mechanism-aware → solution-aware → product-aware. Walk the reader through the exact belief shift.
- **Urgency tied to the mechanism**: "Peru has only 4M alpacas. Very high risk of selling out." Scarcity baked into the story, not fake countdown timers.
- **Review wall matched to medical conditions**: DVT, edema, neuropathy. Selling relief, not socks.

## Inflow (ADHD app)

30+ questions, identity-driven. DR web-app masters. The Funnel Professor calls these "the forefathers of quiz funnels" - more elegant + interactive than DTC quizzes typically are.

**Stealable:**

- **Tension arc over product funnel**: Behaviors first → Identity second → Shame third. User tells themselves the story. By the email gate they've ALREADY told themselves "I have ADHD. Nobody gets it. I need help." Inflow didn't say any of that. The user did.
- **Mascot reassurance mid-quiz**: Quinn drops in after emotionally heavy answers - "That's a lot on your plate, it's okay to want a little breathing room." Breaks clinical coldness. Most quizzes feel like forms; this one feels like a friend checking in.
- **Format rotation prevents 30+ question fatigue**. Yes/no → multi-select → emoji scales → 1-5 ratings → statement agreement. Not for fun - for fatigue prevention. Format switching keeps the brain engaged because each slide feels like a new experience.
- **Education drops reframing shame as neurology**: introduce clinical concepts mid-quiz. "Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria" gives a name to lifetime shame. Then after a procrastination question, Quinn explains executive dysfunction: "With ADHD, you can want to do something so badly and still be unable to do it." Massive tension release + trust built.
- **Positioning as journey, not screening**: Quinn literally says "I'll show up to help as you begin this journey of self-discovery." Not a test. A journey. That framing lets them go 30+ questions deep without drop-off because the user isn't filling out a form - they're learning about themselves.
- **Email gate as self-care, not conversion**: after all that emotional excavation, the email gate isn't a transaction. It's "claim what you've earned." The Funnel Professor: "That's the difference between a quiz that segments and a quiz that sells."

## Kivo Walking ("Asian Walking" for menopausal women)

Mid-funnel walking program targeting women 40+. Quiz-first acquisition, conversational ad framing.

**Stealable:**

- **Conversational ad framing**: ads run as overheard dialogue ("Wow, you're in incredible shape." / "Honestly, I don't have a routine"). Viewer feels like they're eavesdropping, not being sold to. Handles the busy-life objection in the first 2 lines.
- **Two positioning pillars hammered everywhere**: "9 minutes a day" and "no gym, no equipment". These two phrases handle the entire objection stack before the quiz even starts.
- **Identity-first segmentation with image**: Q1 is age, but the image shows a fit, achievable woman in the target bracket. She's proof "women like you" can look like this. Image does heavy lifting before any question is asked.
- **Visual self-identification with body-type cards**: instead of asking "are you overweight?", show 4 neutral, realistic body-shape illustrations and let them click. Self-categorization without the shame of typing "yes I'm overweight". Truth-telling creates ownership.
- **Temporal pain-point question**: "When did you last feel happy with your body?" with a "more than 5 years ago" option that hurts to admit. Creates the emotional gap your product fills. The "Never" option catches people who feel excluded by transformation messaging and reframes it ("your best is still ahead").
- **BMI as data collection disguised as value delivery**: user enters weight, instantly sees "Your BMI is 27.8 which is considered overweight. You have some work ahead, but it's great that you're taking this first step." Validates the decision to take the quiz. Softened language ("some work ahead" not "you're fat") keeps them engaged.
- **Reframe conventional wisdom as objection handling**: dedicated info slide "Forget 10,000 steps. Starting small is how all change begins." Removes a barrier (the user thinks "I can't do 10k steps a day") before it becomes a quit-reason. Echoes the ad's "9 minutes" positioning.
- **Loading screen with named testimonial + customer count**: at 50% loading, show "395,000 people have chosen Kivo" + Anna's quote covering "I used to overcomplicate fitness... now I just follow the plan". Pure social proof while the user can't click away.
- **Personalized profile with positive identity markers**: result page labels them "Mesomorph" and "Intermediate". These are not insults but flattering self-labels. Visual BMI gauge shows current vs target. They feel SEEN, not judged.
- **Specific date forecast tied to a captured event**: "By September 20, 2026 you'll be at 75 kg" + reference to "the wedding" they mentioned earlier. Losing 15kg is abstract; losing 15kg by the wedding is a story. Captured-and-callback is the highest-leverage personalization move.
- **Pricing page = full trigger stack**: countdown timer + "75% off" + before/after comparison + tiered pricing + daily-cost reframe ("$0.33 per day, less than a coffee") + social proof for longer commitment ("3-month plan = double the results of 1-month").

## Muscle Booster (men's muscle gain, 36-step quiz)

Long-form quiz for cold men's-fitness traffic. Sister brand to Kivo (same publisher).

**Stealable:**

- **Couples imagery on age question**: each age bracket shows a fit couple, not a solo model. Frames fitness as relationship currency without saying it. Subtle but works on men.
- **36-step counter signals seriousness**: progress bar showing "4/36" tells the user "this is a real assessment, not a Buzzfeed quiz". Long quiz = perceived expertise. Sunk cost kicks in by step 4.
- **Torso-only body-type images**: cropped to the torso, no face. Lower friction to self-identify ("that's me") because there's no face to compare to.
- **Multi-emotion goal options**: span health / vanity / performance / sex on the same question. Different men buy for different reasons. Routing later screens by goal lets you write 4 different funnels under one quiz.
- **Visual problem-area selection with body diagram**: clicking "Belly" highlights it in blue on a body figure. Physical involvement turns the problem tangible — YOUR belly, right there on screen. Multi-select stacks problems = bigger perceived need for solution.
- **"Full body" escape option** on body-area question: catches max-intent users without forcing them to click every single area.
- **Info-slide as emotional recalibration**: after asking man to admit body type AND point at problems (vulnerable double-hit), drop a slide showing 3 body types standing as equals. Reduces shame + pressure that would cause abandonment. Info slides aren't filler — they're pressure valves.
- **Subtext on assessment questions**: "We ask about fitness level because [reason]". When users understand WHY, they answer more honestly. Honesty deepens commitment.
- **Universal-test fitness assessment**: "How many flights of stairs can you climb without pause?" Everyone has done this. Reveals cardiovascular health without equipment or shame.
- **Color-coded fitness-score reveal**: 40/90 with red bars in Consistency / Strength / Endurance categories. The "oh shit" moment — but framed as "Great for a start!" so they don't bail. Three sub-scores create three specific gaps your product fills.
- **"Our Recommendation" badge on middle option**: when asking workout frequency, badge the 2-day option. Removes choice paralysis + signals expertise + nudges toward an achievable commitment.
- **Yellow → green color signaling**: BMI warning slide uses yellow alarm color. Goal-weight slide uses green. The user feels the journey from "you have a problem" to "you're on the right track" via color, not just copy.

## Spartan ($10M/mo men's hair care, 5-section logical flow)

Hair-loss product to commodity audience in saturated category. Quiz turns the offer page into the obvious next click.

**Stealable:**

- **5-section logical flow** that other quiz frameworks don't make explicit (see Framework 5 in arc-and-phases.md): **Diagnosis → Aspiration Bridge → Personalization → Pre-Framing → Solution**.
- **Section 1 (Diagnosis) = 5 questions only**: age / location of pain / specific symptoms / severity / when it started. Tight problem-mapping without bloat. Every answer gets used on the sales page.
- **Aspiration Bridge as immediate pivot**: right after diagnosis, ONE question — "What results are you expecting?" Pivot from pain to outcome, then drop social proof connecting their specific pain to your specific solution.
- **Personalization as stealth qualification**: section 3 asks 2 questions (hair type, hair length) that LOOK like personalization but actually qualify the SKU recommendation. Same trick works for any product that comes in variants (concentration, flavor, size, etc.).
- **Pre-Framing pre-handles objections inside the funnel**: ask a behavior question ("How much time do you spend on this daily?") then immediately drop an info card defusing the time objection. Then ask causation questions (genetics, stress) which double as objection-handling AND data collection.
- **Section 5 = clinical-sounding diagnosis**: synthesize all answers into a single conclusion stating the user's profile. Then make a time-bound promise: "Based on your answers, we expect visible results by [current month + 6]". Anchors the user to a 6-month supply, not 1-month — that's how AOV gets framed inside the quiz before the offer page renders.
- **Yes-ladder right before the offer**: 2 yes/no commitment gates ("Are you determined to stop this?" / "Does this routine sound good to you?"). By the time they hit submit they've spent 5-10 minutes earning their result. They feel understood. Buying is the consistent next action.
- **Sales page echoes diagnosis**: "Hair Profile Matched. Here's Your Growth Plan." Recommend the 6-month supply (already framed for inside the quiz).
- **Checkout pressure stack**: limited inventory + "card reserved for 10 minutes" + doubled guarantee. The 10-minute reservation is the clever one — feels like the system is holding their spot, not a fake countdown timer.

## The meta-pattern across all breakdowns

Every high-performing quiz funnel:

1. Opens with zero-friction (age, gender, goal) to start the yes-chain
2. Uses multi-select symptom questions as the conversion weapon
3. Has at least one interstitial that reframes competing solutions (new mechanism positioning)
4. Escalates from neutral → emotional → identity
5. Uses real time pressure (biological, event-based) instead of fake scarcity
6. Ends with 1-2 commitment-gate questions before the offer
7. Shows a personalized pseudo-diagnosis on the results page
8. Offers stacked value with a $0 or near-free entry and a clear upsell path
