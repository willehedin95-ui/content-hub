# Patterns — Interstitials, Loaders, Results, Offers

The non-question slides that tie a quiz together. As important as the questions themselves.

---

## Insight panels (interstitials)

One-slide breaks between questions. No question on the slide — just a statement and a "Continue" button. Rule of thumb: one insight panel per 4-5 questions.

### Pattern A: The Pattern Reveal

After a cluster of symptom questions:

> "What you're describing isn't random. When [symptom A], [symptom B], and [symptom C] show up together, it usually points to [mechanism]."

Turns scattered symptoms into a single coherent narrative. Reduces confusion, lowers self-blame, plants the mechanism.

### Pattern B: The Normalization

After a heavy admission:

> "Most people think this is just [normal thing]. It's not. It's common — but it's also fixable once you know what's actually happening."

Separates "common" from "normal." Introduces possibility. Releases pressure.

### Pattern C: The Competitive Destruction (new mechanism positioning)

This is the single highest-leverage interstitial in any quiz.

> "Most [treatments/products] stay on the surface. They treat the symptom, not the cause. Our [mechanism] goes to the root."

One slide. Every competitor destroyed without naming a single one. The reader makes the connection themselves, which is always more persuasive than a comparison chart.

Transferable to any market:
- **Supplements**: "Most multivitamins are synthetic and your body can't absorb them."
- **Skincare**: "Moisturizers treat the symptom, not the cause."
- **Shoes**: "Most 'comfortable' shoes are actually weakening your feet."
- **SaaS**: "You don't have a productivity problem, you have a prioritization problem."

### Pattern D: The Hope Injection

After the negative/cost section:

> "87% of people with [your condition] report being more [positive trait] than average. Your brain is wired for this. It just needs the right tool."

Reframes the flaw as a superpower. Releases pressure after cost expansion.

### Pattern E: The Social Proof Mid-Quiz

After the first aspirational question (before the hard questions):

> "Great choice. 100,000+ people who selected [their goal] have successfully [outcome]."

Bandwagon effect activated BEFORE the heavy lifting begins. Trust enables vulnerability.

### Pattern F: The Educational Authority (Dr. Brand, etc.)

Single slide with an authority figure and a clinical claim:

> "**Dr. [Name], 20 years as a [specialist]:** 'Most people are missing [mechanism] — and that's why [standard solutions] fail.'"

Borrows authority. Frames your product as the clinical answer.

## Loading screens (the captive attention weapon)

The "Analyzing your results..." screen. Most brands waste it with a spinning wheel. Don't.

### What a good loading screen contains

During 10-15 seconds of captive attention, show:

- **Massive testimonial**: one before/after with a specific claim ("Lost 47 lbs in 3 months")
- **Social proof stats**: ticker of numbers ("Join 50,000+ who transformed their skin")
- **Product preview**: fade-in glimpse so it feels familiar when they see the offer
- **Progressive task list**: "Analyzing your answers ✓ / Matching to our formula ✓ / Personalizing your plan ✓"

### Rule

The user CANNOT click away. They're waiting for their results. Use every second.

### Implementation tip

Don't use a single long loading screen. Break into 2-3 shorter loaders (5-7 seconds each) interspersed with different content so the brain doesn't register "this is stalling."

## The Profile Trifecta (the close) - Mars Men 3-Card System

Three-slide sequence at the end of a quiz. High-converting on physical-condition products. Originally from app onboarding, now standard in DTC. Mars Men ($100M+ men's health) refined the canonical implementation.

### Slide 1: The Profile Page (how cooked you are)

All their answers summarized. Show severity. Clinical visuals. Color-coded alarm levels.

> **Your profile:** [Key name their condition]
> Score: 32/100 — Critical zone
> Duration: 2+ years
> Impact areas: Energy, focus, sleep quality

**The job:** create urgency + fear. Show them how "cooked" they are. The user looks at this card and thinks "shit, this is worse than I thought."

This is the **doctor's diagnosis** moment - the user trusts the prescription because they trust the process that led to it.

### Slide 2: The Future-Pacing Page (where you can be)

Where they could be with the right intervention. Graphs work exceptionally well here.

> "By [specific date 90 days out], 87% of people with your profile report [specific outcome]"

**Specificity is non-negotiable on the date** (e.g. "February 6", not "in 90 days"). Specific dates feel like forecasts, not vague promises.

Split the graph into two lines: "Current trajectory (no change)" vs "With intervention" - the visual contrast does the selling. Adding a red warning band on the do-nothing trajectory amplifies fear of inaction.

For event-driven funnels: reference the user's captured event ("by your wedding on June 5"). Captured-and-callback is the highest-leverage personalization move.

### Slide 3: The Offer Page (perfect for you)

Based on the answers, their personalized recommendation.

> "Based on your profile, we built this for you..."

**The trick:** add a final micro-loading screen confirming "sourcing your product..." or "claiming your offer..." right before the offer page renders. 3-5 seconds. The anticipation peak hits the offer at maximum desire.

By this point the user has been through 15+ questions, seen their profile diagnosed, watched themselves projected into the future with intervention. They don't question the prescription. They trust it.

> "You don't question the doctor when he writes your prescription."
> -- The Funnel Professor on Mars Men

## The "Take Quiz" CTA pattern (Happy Mammoth)

Most brands run ads like:

```
Ad → Product Page (Buy Now)
```

Higher-converting brands run:

```
Ad → "Take Quiz" / "Get Your Free Diagnosis" → Quiz → Offer
```

### Why this works

**Customers love taking quizzes.** Frame the ad CTA as "Get Your Free Diagnosis" / "Find Your [X] Profile" / "Take the 2-Minute Assessment" instead of "Shop Now."

**You're giving them value, not extracting it.**

The goal of an ad is to **get the click**, not sell. A quiz CTA gets cheaper clicks because:

- The promise is information, not a transaction
- The user feels they're getting a free diagnostic tool, not being sold
- Click-through rate climbs (lower CPM)
- Quiz completion → offer page CVR is 3-5%+ vs 1-2% on direct product page

### How to position the entry

Weak: "Take our quiz"

Strong:
- "Get your personalized [X] diagnosis (2 minutes)"
- "Find your [X] profile"
- "Take this 1-minute quiz to claim your discount"
- "Discover what's causing your [problem]"

Frame it as **free value** they're receiving, not effort they're donating.

### When NOT to use

Solution-aware audiences who already want to buy can be sent direct to product pages. The quiz CTA works best for **problem-aware to solution-aware** segments (Schwartz Stage 2-3). They have a problem but haven't committed to a solution yet.

## "Did You Know" stat opener

Pattern for quiz entry pages OR offer pages. Open with a counter-intuitive or eye-catching statistic that:

1. Makes the user pause (interrupts scroll/scan)
2. Promises a specific outcome
3. Sets up the case for your product

Examples:

- "Did you know: 82% of users who used our app for 2 weeks saw improvements in their dog's behavior?" (Dogo)
- "65% of users started their training with Woofz and advanced in their goals within the first month*" (Woofz)
- "98% of our users notice positive changes in their dog's behavior just 1 week after starting" (EveryDoggy)
- "Over 200 000 books already ordered!" + 4.8/5 stars (Raising.dog)

### The structure

```
"Did you know: [counter-intuitive or impressive stat]"
* [small disclaimer about source if needed]
```

The asterisk-disclaimer makes the stat feel rigorous, not made up. Even if the source is "based on user-reported survey data," cite it.

### Where to place

- **Quiz entry page** (positions the funnel as backed by data)
- **Loading screen** (captive attention - drop the stat there)
- **Offer page hero** (anchors the offer in scientific-feeling claims)
- **Email subject lines** ("Did you know 82%...")

## Commitment gates (two-yes close)

Placed immediately before the offer. Two yes/no questions, both designed so "yes" is the natural answer.

1. "Are you ready to finally [outcome]?"
2. "Can you commit to [specific action, 30 seconds / 1 capsule / daily]?"

After clicking yes twice, saying no to the offer is psychologically inconsistent with what the user just committed to. 20-30% CVR lift in many tests.

## Offer page patterns

Not a quiz slide itself, but the quiz's payoff. Key elements:

### Personalization mirror

> "Sarah, based on your answers you need [product] for [trail hiking/combo skin/daily energy]."

Reference the user's specific answers. Headline. Hero image. Testimonial pick.

### Stacked value

- Product: $X value
- Bonus 1 ("Free Beauty Guide"): $Y value
- Bonus 2 ("Quiz Completer Discount"): $Z off
- Free shipping / free returns / money-back guarantee

### Insane offer mechanics (Spoiled Child model)

- **$0 trial** → billed $55 after 30 days if kept
- **Buy 2 get 2 free** with scarcity ("87% sold, Peru only has 4M alpacas")
- **Subscribe and save** ("Free first month")
- **Expedited shipping upsell** (+$14.95 at checkout)

### Split-route offers per quiz answer

Users who selected goal A land on offer page A with headline tuned to goal A. Users who selected goal B land on offer page B. Same product, different framing. Big CVR + AOV bump.

### Final confirmation loader

A small loader between "Claim my offer" and the checkout page. Feels like the system is setting up their personalized bundle. Micro-delay that peaks anticipation before checkout.

## Position the quiz with a promise

Before users enter the quiz, they need to know what they'll get.

Weak positioning: "Take our quiz"

Strong positioning:
- "Take this 1-minute quiz to claim your discount"
- "Find your skin type in 60 seconds"
- "Get your free personalized plan"
- "Discover what's causing your [problem]"

Frame it as free value they're receiving, not effort they're donating.

For ads: "Take Quiz" CTA button gets cheaper clicks than "Shop Now." Customers love taking quizzes. Use that.
